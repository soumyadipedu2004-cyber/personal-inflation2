Methodology

How your personal rate is calculated

A personal inflation rate is not a different statistic from the official CPI — it is the same category-level price data, re-weighted by a real household's actual budget instead of the fixed weights used to build the national average.

01

Record your monthly spend

Enter approximate monthly amounts across the twelve categories MoSPI uses to construct the CPI, and choose a rural, urban, or combined price reference for your area.

02

Weight the published price movements

Each category's official year-on-year rate is multiplied by its share of your own budget, rather than the fixed national weight used in the headline figure.

03

Compare against the national CPI

The weighted sum is your personal inflation rate. It is shown next to the national figure, alongside the single category doing the most to move it.

Why a household rate can differ from the headline figure

The national CPI weights are built from average consumption patterns across millions of households, surveyed periodically by MoSPI. No single household spends in exactly those proportions. A household that spends heavily on housing and healthcare, and lightly on tobacco and transport, will experience a different blended rate than the headline number — even though both are built from the same category-level prices.

What stays fixed, and what doesn't

The twelve category-level rates are fixed inputs, published by MoSPI for a given month and region. What changes from household to household is only the weighting — the proportion of the monthly budget spent in each category. That single difference is enough to produce meaningfully different personal rates across households in the same city and month.

The formula

The exact calculation, step by step

Work out what share of your budget each category takes up, multiply that share by the category's published rate, and sum the results. Below is the formula used by the calculator, followed by a worked example.

Step 1 — Category weight
weighti = expenditurei ÷ total expenditure
Step 2 — Category contribution
contributioni = weighti × ratei
Step 3 — Personal inflation rate
personal rate = Σ contributioni   (i = 1 … 12 categories)
Step 4 — Comparison to the headline figure
difference = personal rate − national CPI
i
One of the twelve CPI categories — Food & Groceries, Housing & Utilities, Transport, and so on.
expenditurei
The amount you enter for that category in the calculator, in rupees per month.
ratei
The published year-on-year price change for that category, from the MoSPI CPI release for your selected region.
weighti
The share of your total monthly spend that category represents — this is what makes the rate "personal" rather than national.
Σ
Sum across all twelve categories.

Practical guidance

Managing your personal inflation rate

A personal rate is only useful if it changes what you do next. The following are general, widely-used approaches to reducing exposure to rising prices — not tailored financial advice.

Run the calculator above and this panel will rank exactly which of your expenses is doing the most to raise your personal rate — by name, not just by category type.

Your biggest contributors

Ranked by how many percentage points each category adds to your personal rate. Start controlling spend at the top of this list — it moves the number the most.

01

Revisit your largest contributor first

The category identified as your largest contributor is doing the most damage. Even a modest reduction there moves your personal rate more than cuts anywhere else.

02

Separate essential spend from discretionary spend

Housing, food, and healthcare are hard to compress. Dining out, recreation, and personal care usually have more room — trim there before cutting essentials.

03

Rebalance toward slower-rising categories

If two categories serve a similar need, shifting spend toward the one with a lower published rate reduces your weighted average without reducing your standard of living.

04

Review recurring costs annually

Subscriptions, insurance premiums, and utility plans rarely get renegotiated. An annual review against current market rates often uncovers savings that a one-off cut in groceries cannot match.

05

Keep an emergency buffer ahead of essentials inflation

When food and fuel are the fastest-rising categories, a cash buffer of three to six months of essential spend absorbs price shocks without forcing debt.

06

Track the number, not just the headline

Recalculate your personal rate every few months. A rate that is consistently above the national CPI is a signal to revisit the budget mix, not a one-off anomaly.

A note on limits This calculator is a budgeting aid, not investment or tax advice. For decisions involving savings, loans, or investments, consider speaking with a qualified financial advisor who can account for your full circumstances.
About the numbers

Where the data comes from

Your personal inflation rate uses the same category-level price movements published in the Consumer Price Index, but weights them by your own budget instead of the fixed national weights used to build the headline figure. Two households in the same city and month can face materially different inflation simply because they buy different things in different proportions.

Category-level rates shown here are the Provisional Consumer Price Index figures for May 2026, as published by the Ministry of Statistics and Programme Implementation (MoSPI), broken out by rural, urban, and combined index.

This tool is intended for personal budgeting insight, not financial advice. Figures are provisional and subject to revision by MoSPI; verify against the official release before relying on them for decisions.
